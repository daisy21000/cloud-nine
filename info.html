Common Mental Health Issues

Stress

Stress is the body’s natural reaction to challenges, demands, or perceived threats. It can be triggered by external pressures like work, relationships, or major life changes, as well as internal factors such as self-expectations or unresolved emotions. While stress can sometimes help us stay alert and focused, prolonged or intense stress may lead to physical symptoms like headaches, fatigue, and sleep disturbances, and can affect emotional well-being over time.

Find out more on the NHS website

Anxiety

Anxiety is characterized by persistent feelings of worry, nervousness, or fear that may not always have a clear cause. It can affect how a person thinks, feels, and behaves, and may include physical symptoms such as rapid heartbeat, muscle tension, or difficulty breathing. Anxiety can be general or specific to certain situations, and it often interferes with daily life, decision-making, and social interactions.

Find out more on the NHS website

Depression

Depression is a mood disorder that involves ongoing feelings of sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness. It can affect a person’s ability to enjoy life, concentrate, and carry out everyday tasks. People experiencing depression may notice changes in sleep, appetite, energy levels, and self-esteem. It’s a complex condition that varies from person to person, and it often involves emotional, cognitive, and physical symptoms.

Find out more on the NHS website

Eating Disorders

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions that involve disrupted eating behaviours and intense concerns about body weight, shape, or food. These disorders can include restrictive eating, binge eating, purging, or obsessive thoughts about food and exercise. They often stem from a combination of psychological, social, and biological factors, and can have significant effects on physical health and emotional well-being.

Find out more on the NHS website

Personality Disorders

Personality disorders are patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that differ significantly from cultural expectations and can cause distress or difficulty in relationships and daily functioning. These patterns are typically long-lasting and may affect how individuals perceive themselves and others, regulate emotions, and respond to stress. There are several types of personality disorders, each with its own traits and challenges.

Find out more on the NHS website

Phobias

Phobias are intense, irrational fears of specific objects, activities, or situations — such as heights, animals, or public speaking. These fears often lead to avoidance behaviours and can cause significant distress when the feared subject is encountered or even anticipated. Phobias are more than simple discomfort; they can interfere with daily routines, social life, and overall sense of safety.

Find out more on the NHS website
